Clubfoot - Musculoskeletal System

All Clubfoot


Clubfoot, also known as congenital talipes equinovarus, is a congenital foot deformity characterized by abnormal positioning of one or both feet, typically presenting with the foot turned inward and downward. It is among the most common musculoskeletal birth defects, with an incidence of approximately 1 per 1,000 live births. The exact etiology remains unknown, though genetic and environmental factors are believed to play a role. Clubfoot is diagnosed by ultrasound during pregnancy, through physical examination after birth. It is Managed by a sequential series of manipulations and casting according to the Ponseti method, aimed at gradually correcting the foot deformity.



Overview



Clubfoot is a congenital musculoskeletal condition characterized by abnormal positioning and alignment of the foot and ankle at birth. Typically known as congenital talipes equinovarus (CTEV), clubfoot manifests as a combination of inward rotation and downward pointing of the foot, leading to difficulties in weight-bearing and normal movement. This condition affects the bones, joints, ligaments, and muscles of the foot and ankle, resulting in stiffness, rigidity, and limited mobility. Clubfoot is often detected during prenatal ultrasounds or immediately after birth. The exact cause of clubfoot is not fully understood, but it is believed to involve a combination of genetic and environmental factors. While the majority of clubfoot cases occur without any known cause (idiopathic clubfoot), it can also be associated with certain genetic conditions or intrauterine factors. Early intervention is crucial in managing clubfoot to prevent long-term complications and optimize outcomes. Treatment typically involves a series of gentle manipulations, stretching exercises, and casting techniques to gradually reposition the foot and correct its alignment. In some cases, minor surgical procedures may be necessary to release tight tendons or ligaments. With timely and appropriate treatment, the majority of children born with clubfoot can achieve near-normal foot function and mobility. However, without intervention, clubfoot can lead to significant disability and limitations in mobility. Therefore, early diagnosis, prompt intervention, and comprehensive multidisciplinary care are essential in addressing clubfoot and ensuring optimal musculoskeletal development and function.

Pathophysiology


Clubfoot, a congenital musculoskeletal anomaly, involves complex pathophysiological mechanisms primarily affecting the bones, joints, ligaments, and muscles of the foot and ankle. While the exact etiology remains incompletely understood, clubfoot is believed to result from a combination of genetic predisposition and intrauterine environmental factors influencing fetal development. In clubfoot, the foot is typically positioned in a fixed inward and downward orientation, known as equinovarus deformity. This abnormal positioning is thought to arise from aberrant muscle and tendon development during fetal gestation, leading to imbalance and contracture of the foot structures. Histological studies have shown abnormalities in the composition and organization of muscle fibers, tendons, ligaments, and joint capsules in clubfoot, suggesting disruptions in normal tissue development and maturation. Additionally, alterations in the biomechanical forces acting on the foot and ankle during embryogenesis may contribute to the malformation. While clubfoot can occur as an isolated anomaly, it may also be associated with other congenital conditions or syndromes, further complicating its pathophysiology. These syndromic forms of clubfoot often involve additional musculoskeletal and systemic abnormalities, reflecting the broader impact of genetic and developmental factors. Timely intervention is essential in addressing clubfoot to prevent long-term functional impairment and disability. Treatment typically involves a combination of gentle manipulation, stretching exercises, serial casting, and in some cases, surgical correction to realign the foot and restore normal anatomy. Through comprehensive multidisciplinary care and early intervention, the majority of individuals with clubfoot can achieve satisfactory outcomes and functional mobility.

Signs, symptoms, causes and risk factors of clubfoot.



What are the signs and symptoms of clubfoot?


Symptoms of Clubfoot - Musculoskeletal System include:

  • Abnormal positioning of the foot, the heel turned inward and the foot pointing downward.
  • The affected foot may also appear smaller.
  • Higher arch than normal (called cavus foot deformity).
  • Lack of full range of motion in their foot.
  • crease on the inside.
  • Shorter foot.


    • What causes clubfoot?


    There is no known cause of clubfoot, but there are different factors that raise the risk of having a baby with clubfoot. The followings are some of risk factors for congenital clubfoot.

    Risk factors for congenital clubfoot?

  • Gender males are affected by clubfoot more twice time than females.
  • Genetic factors:
  • Family history: a family history of clubfoot also puts your baby at a higher risk.
  • Another birth defect: babies are also at a higher risk if they have another birth defect like spina bifida or cerebral palsy.
  • Genetic condition: babies are also at a higher risk if they have a genetic condition, such as Trisomy 18 (Edward syndrome).
  • Had oligohydramnios during pregnancy. This is a problem of not having enough amniotic fluid, the fluid that surrounds the fetus.
  • Had Zika infection during pregnancy, , which can lead to birth defects and other issues.
  • Smoked , drank alcohol or used recreational drugs during pregnancy, may raise the risk of having a baby with a birth defect like clubfoot.


    • Diagnosis and Tests



    How is clubfoot diagnosed?


    Clubfoot can be diagnosed before birth or after birth. During pregnancy, clubfoot can be diagnosed by using Ultrasound. A prenatal ultrasound shows pictures of the developing fetus. If your provider diagnoses clubfoot during pregnancy, you can start planning for the early treatment after birth. Other times, health care provider diagnoses clubfoot after birth, during babyâ€™s first physical exams. In some cases, your provider may recommend an X-ray to confirm the diagnosis.

    • 1.Ponseti method


    The Ponseti method is a widely used and highly effective non-surgical approach for the management of clubfoot. Developed by Dr. Ignacio Ponseti, this method involves a series of gentle manipulations and casting to gradually correct the position of the foot over several weeks. The followings are steps in correction phase in ponsenti method.

  • Find the Lateral Head of Talus
  • Hold the foot.
  • Correction of Cavus
  • Correction of equinus.
  • Automatic Correction of Varus
  • Correction of adductus.
  • Casting.

    2.Physical examination


    The Ponseti method typically begins with physical examination to assess the severity of clubfoot, which includes assessing the flexibility of the foot, the range of motion of the joints, and any associated abnormalities.



    3.Manipulation:


    Ponseti method involves gentle manipulation to stretch tight ligaments and tendons and guide the foot into a more normal.



    4.Casting:


    After manipulation, the foot is placed in a plaster cast to maintain the corrected position. The cast is usually changed weekly as the foot gradually responds to the correction. The casting process continues until the foot is in the desired position



    4.Achilles tenotomy:


    In some cases, a minor surgical procedure called Achilles tenotomy may be performed to release the tight Achilles tendon and allow further correction of the foot. This procedure is typically performed under local anesthesia and involves a small incision to release the tendon.



    5.Foot brace/ special shoes:


    Once the foot is corrected, bracing is essential to maintain the corrected position and prevent recurrence of the clubfoot deformity. The child is typically fitted with special braces (commonly known as Ponseti braces or boots and bar) that are worn full-time initially and then gradually reduced to nighttime use as the child grows.

    Complications of clubfoot

    Untreated clubfoot can lead to:

  • Walking problems. Babies with clubfoot often walk in unusual ways. Typically, people walk on the bottoms and soles of their feet, but babies with clubfoot may walk on the sides and tops of their feet.
  • Foot infections.
  • Foot problems, including calluses. A callus is a thick layer of skin that often develops on the sole of the foot.
  • Arthrita joint condition that causes pain, stiffness and swelling.
  • Movement. The affected foot may be slightly less flexible
  • Movement. The affected foot may be slightly less flexible
  • Leg length. The affected foot may be slightly less flexible
  • Shoe size. The affected foot may be up to 1 1/2 shoe sizes smaller than the unaffected foot.
  • Calf size. The muscles of the calf on the affected side may always be smaller than those on the other side.

    • Prognosis


    With early and appropriate treatment, the prognosis for clubfoot is generally excellent. Most children with clubfoot can achieve near-normal function and appearance of the affected foot with proper treatment. Babies who start treatment early have good results. They can wear regular shoes, walk, run and play without pain. They can even play sports.

    Can clubfoot return?

    There can be a recurrence of clubfoot which is more likely to happen if the treatment schedule wasnâ€™t followed correctly. If the foot returns to the clubfoot position, see a physiotherapist or any other healthcare provider. They can advise you on the next steps. You may need to repeat some stages of the treatment plan.

    How can I help my child with his/her brace?

    Regularly wearing the brace gives your child the best chances for success. But it can be challenging for children to wear the brace for so many hours a day. These tips can help parents ease the process of brace-wearing:

    • Make it fun: Play with your child when theyâ€™re wearing their brace. Do gentle exercises and kicking games. Use the bar to help bend and straighten their knees.
    • Protect yourself and the fetus against Zika virus by getting checked regularly.

    • Add a pad: A soft pad on the metal bar makes the brace more comfortable for your child and yourself. It also has the benefit of protecting furniture and appliances in your house.
    • Prevent slippage: Sometimes, the foot keeps slipping out of the brace. Make sure the strap is tight. Double socks may also help keep the shoe firmly on the foot. A physical therapist can recommend other steps to make sure the shoe is snug on the foot.
